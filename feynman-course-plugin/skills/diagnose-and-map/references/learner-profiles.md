# Learner Profiles Reference

This document describes the 5 learner levels used by the Feynman course plugin to calibrate teaching depth, pacing, and content complexity.

---

## Beginner

No prior exposure. Needs everything from scratch. Focus on "what is this" and "why does it matter". Use everyday analogies. Assume zero jargon knowledge.

- **Typical prerequisite state**: No familiarity with the topic or its terminology. May not even know the field exists or why anyone cares about it.
- **Common misconceptions**: Often conflates the topic with something superficially similar. May have pop-culture-level misunderstandings. Tends to assume the topic is either trivially simple or impossibly complex.
- **Recommended teaching depth**: Surface - establish vocabulary, orientation, and motivation before any mechanics.
- **Lesson count suggestion**: 3 lessons - just enough to cover what, why, and a first taste of how.
- **Visual complexity**: Simple - one concept per visual, minimal labels, large text, no more than 5 elements.

---

## Novice

Has heard of the topic, maybe tried it once. Has surface understanding but major gaps. Focus on building mental models and correcting early misconceptions.

- **Typical prerequisite state**: Knows the name and maybe one or two facts. Has attempted the topic casually but lacks structure. Can recognize terms but not define them accurately.
- **Common misconceptions**: Overgeneralizes from limited experience. Confuses correlation with causation within the domain. Often has one "trick" they think covers everything.
- **Recommended teaching depth**: Working - build functional mental models that can support future learning. Correct wrong models before building new ones.
- **Lesson count suggestion**: 4 lessons - enough to build a coherent foundation and connect the dots between core ideas.
- **Visual complexity**: Simple to moderate - up to 7 elements, labeled relationships, one level of hierarchy.

---

## Intermediate

Understands basics, has some practice. Gaps in connecting concepts and applying to novel situations. Focus on "how things connect" and deeper mechanisms.

- **Typical prerequisite state**: Can explain the basics to someone else (roughly). Has used the knowledge in practice a few times. Knows the key terms but may not fully understand the principles behind them.
- **Common misconceptions**: Thinks they understand more than they do ("illusion of competence"). Applies rules without understanding when they break down. Struggles to transfer knowledge to unfamiliar contexts.
- **Recommended teaching depth**: Deep - reveal the mechanisms behind the rules. Show when and why exceptions exist. Connect islands of knowledge into a coherent map.
- **Lesson count suggestion**: 5 lessons - enough depth to cover connections, exceptions, and application to new scenarios.
- **Visual complexity**: Moderate - multi-node concept maps, process flows with branching, comparison diagrams with 3+ items.

---

## Advanced

Strong working knowledge. Needs edge cases, optimization, trade-offs, and advanced patterns. Focus on nuance and expert-level distinctions.

- **Typical prerequisite state**: Can teach the basics to others. Has significant practical experience. Understands most standard patterns but may not know the "why" behind all of them.
- **Common misconceptions**: Assumes best practices are universal rather than contextual. Underestimates edge cases. May have blind spots in areas adjacent to their experience. Conflates familiarity with mastery.
- **Recommended teaching depth**: Expert - focus on trade-off analysis, edge cases, optimization strategies, and the reasoning experts use. Challenge existing mental models.
- **Lesson count suggestion**: 6 lessons - enough to cover advanced patterns, edge cases, trade-offs, and synthesis across sub-topics.
- **Visual complexity**: Detailed - system diagrams with multiple interacting components, decision matrices, trade-off visualizations with multiple dimensions.

---

## Expert

Deep knowledge, seeking mastery-level refinement. Needs cutting-edge developments, contrarian perspectives, and teaching-level synthesis. Focus on "what most people get wrong" and frontier knowledge.

- **Typical prerequisite state**: Could write a textbook chapter on the topic. Has years of experience and strong intuitions. Seeks the gaps they do not know they have.
- **Common misconceptions**: May be anchored to outdated models. Can confuse personal experience with universal truth. May dismiss alternative approaches without full evaluation. Sometimes over-complicates when simplicity would serve better.
- **Recommended teaching depth**: Expert - present frontier research, contrarian viewpoints, cross-domain analogies, and meta-cognitive insights. Focus on what distinguishes good practitioners from great ones.
- **Lesson count suggestion**: 7 lessons - enough to cover frontier knowledge, contrarian perspectives, cross-domain connections, and teaching-level synthesis.
- **Visual complexity**: Detailed - multi-layered system diagrams, causal networks with feedback loops, comparative frameworks across paradigms.
